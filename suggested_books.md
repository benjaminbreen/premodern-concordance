# Suggested Books for the Premodern Concordance

100 works spanning antiquity to 1900, selected for their significance in shaping knowledge of nature, science, and medicine across languages. Prioritizes texts with rich entity networks (substances, persons, places, diseases, concepts) and cross-referential potential. Organized chronologically.

---

## Ancient & Medieval Foundations (pre-1500)

These are the texts that virtually every early modern author cites. Including them enables upstream linking.

| # | Author | Title | Date | Language | Notes |
|---|--------|-------|------|----------|-------|
| 1 | Pliny the Elder | *Naturalis Historia* | 77 CE | Latin | The ur-text of European natural knowledge. 37 books covering cosmology, geography, zoology, botany, pharmacology, mineralogy. Entity-dense. |
| 2 | Dioscorides | *De Materia Medica* | c. 65 CE | Latin (trans.) | ~600 plants, 35 animal products, 90 minerals. The pharmacological backbone of every later herbal. Circulated in Latin and Italian translations throughout early modern Europe. |
| 3 | Galen | *De Simplicium Medicamentorum Temperamentis ac Facultatibus* | 2nd c. | Latin (trans.) | Systematic classification of simples by their "temperaments." Defines the vocabulary of humoral pharmacology for 1,500 years. Used in Latin translation throughout the early modern period. |
| 4 | Ibn Sina (Avicenna) | *Canon Medicinae* (Latin trans. of *al-Qānūn*) | 1025 / Latin trans. 12th c. | Latin | The organizational framework for university medicine in Europe through the 17th century. The Latin translation by Gerard of Cremona was the standard medical textbook for centuries. Massive entity overlap with Galen and Dioscorides. |
| 5 | Hildegard of Bingen | *Physica* (Liber Subtilitatum) | c. 1150 | Latin | Unusual mix of humoral medicine with Germanic folk botany and personal observation. Rich in substance names not found in Mediterranean traditions. |
| 6 | Albertus Magnus | *De Animalibus* | c. 1260 | Latin | Aristotelian zoology filtered through scholastic method plus firsthand observation. Key bridge between ancient and early modern natural history. |

## 16th Century

| # | Author | Title | Date | Language | Notes |
|---|--------|-------|------|----------|-------|
| 7 | Otto Brunfels | *Herbarum Vivae Eicones* | 1530 | Latin | One of the "fathers of botany." Important for the transition from medieval to observational plant description. |
| 8 | Agrippa von Nettesheim | *De Occulta Philosophia* | 1531–33 | Latin | Encyclopedic synthesis of natural magic, astrology, Neoplatonism. Rich in the conceptual vocabulary of occult qualities and sympathies. |
| 9 | Paracelsus | *Die große Wundarznei* | 1536 | German | Radical break with Galenic pharmacology. Introduces chemical (iatrochemical) terminology—mercury, sulfur, salt as principles—that reshapes substance categories. |
| 10 | Leonhart Fuchs | *De Historia Stirpium* | 1542 | Latin | 500+ plant descriptions with systematic attention to synonyms across languages. Excellent for cross-lingual entity matching. |
| 11 | Andreas Vesalius | *De Humani Corporis Fabrica* | 1543 | Latin | Foundational anatomical text. Introduces hundreds of anatomical terms still in use, many deviating from Galenic nomenclature. |
| 12 | Girolamo Fracastoro | *De Contagione et Contagiosis Morbis* | 1546 | Latin | First systematic theory of contagion via "seeds of disease" (*seminaria*). Disease entity names and transmission concepts. |
| 13 | Gerolamo Cardano | *De Subtilitate* | 1550 | Latin | Polymath's encyclopedia of natural philosophy. Mixes physics, natural history, technology, medicine. |
| 14 | Conrad Gessner | *Historia Animalium* | 1551–58 | Latin | Encyclopedic zoology collecting all ancient and contemporary sources. 4,500+ pages. Extraordinary for cross-referencing person and substance entities. |
| 15 | Pierre Belon | *L'Histoire de la Nature des Oyseaux* | 1555 | French | Comparative anatomy of birds. Notable early use of French (not Latin) for natural history. |
| 16 | Gabriele Falloppio | *Observationes Anatomicae* | 1561 | Latin | Corrections and additions to Vesalius. Source of many anatomical terms (fallopian tubes, etc.). |
| 17 | García de Orta | *Colóquios dos Simples e Drogas da Índia* | 1563 | Portuguese | First European account of Indian materia medica based on direct observation. Mango, cannabis, cholera, bezoar stones. Dialogue form preserving Indian terminology. |
| 18 | Nicolás Monardes | *Historia Medicinal de las Cosas que se Traen de Nuestras Indias Occidentales* | 1565–74 | Spanish | Tobacco, sassafras, guaiacum, coca, balsam of Peru—the New World pharmacopoeia entering European knowledge. Hugely influential. |
| 19 | Jean Fernel | *Universa Medicina* | 1567 | Latin | Coined "physiology" as a discipline name. Systematic reworking of Galenic medicine that was the standard textbook for a century. |
| 20 | Bernardino de Sahagún | *Historia General de las Cosas de Nueva España* (Florentine Codex) | c. 1569 | Spanish | Encyclopedic ethnography of Aztec knowledge including medicine, botany, zoology. The Spanish text is rich in substance, disease, and plant entities drawn from Mesoamerican knowledge. |
| 21 | Ambroise Paré | *Les Œuvres* | 1575 | French | Surgical knowledge in the vernacular. Wound treatment, poisons, monsters, plague. Rich in disease and substance entities. |
| 22 | Giambattista della Porta | *Magia Naturalis* (expanded ed.) | 1589 | Latin | Natural magic as experimental philosophy. Optics, magnetism, agriculture, distillation, cryptography. |
| 23 | José de Acosta | *Historia Natural y Moral de las Indias* | 1590 | Spanish | Natural and human history of the Americas. Altitude sickness, coca, chocolate, quinoa, New World geography. Widely translated. |
| 24 | Prospero Alpini | *De Medicina Aegyptiorum* | 1591 | Latin | Egyptian medical practices observed firsthand. Coffee, opium, balsam, moxa. Important for substance entities entering Europe from the Islamic world. |
| 25 | Pietro Andrea Mattioli | *Commentarii in Sex Libros Pedacii Dioscoridis* | 1544 (Latin); 1568 (Italian expanded ed.) | Italian / Latin | The most widely read herbal of the 16th century—over 30,000 copies sold. Commentary on Dioscorides with hundreds of additions. Translated into French, German, and Czech. Ideal for cross-lingual substance matching. |
| 26 | Carolus Clusius | *Rariorum Plantarum Historia* | 1601 | Latin | Pan-European botany plus exotics. Clusius was the great networker—his plant names and descriptions circulated everywhere. |

## 17th Century

| # | Author | Title | Date | Language | Notes |
|---|--------|-------|------|----------|-------|
| 27 | Ulisse Aldrovandi | *Ornithologiae* / *De Animalibus Insectis* / etc. | 1599–1668 | Latin | Massive multi-volume natural history. 13 folio volumes published posthumously. Entity-saturated. |
| 28 | Santorio Santorio | *Ars de Statica Medicina* | 1614 | Latin | Pioneering quantitative physiology—weighed himself, food, and excreta for 30 years. Birth of metabolic measurement. |
| 29 | Francis Bacon | *Novum Organum* | 1620 | Latin | Foundational text of empirical method. Important for concept entities: induction, experiment, "idols of the mind." |
| 30 | William Harvey | *Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis* | 1628 | Latin | Circulation of the blood. Relatively short but conceptually revolutionary. Dense with anatomical and physiological terms. |
| 31 | Francisco Hernández | *Rerum Medicarum Novae Hispaniae Thesaurus* | pub. 1628 | Latin | 3,000+ Mexican plants. Philip II's natural history of New Spain. The published Latin edition preserves indigenous plant names within a European scholarly framework. |
| 32 | Thomas Browne | *Pseudodoxia Epidemica* | 1646 | English | Encyclopedia of "vulgar errors" in natural knowledge. Reviews and debunks hundreds of natural-historical claims—references virtually every ancient authority. |
| 33 | Jan Baptist van Helmont | *Ortus Medicinae* | 1648 | Latin | Chemical medicine post-Paracelsus. Coined "gas." Ferments, alkahest, willow tree experiment. Bridge between alchemy and chemistry. |
| 34 | Willem Piso & Georg Marcgraf | *Historia Naturalis Brasiliae* | 1648 | Latin | First systematic natural history of Brazil. Tropical medicine, plants, animals. Rich in Tupi terminology. |
| 35 | Robert Boyle | *The Sceptical Chymist* | 1661 | English | Critique of Aristotelian elements and Paracelsian principles. Foundational for chemical terminology. |
| 36 | Marcello Malpighi | *De Pulmonibus* / *Anatome Plantarum* | 1661 / 1675 | Latin | Microscopic anatomy of lungs and plants. Capillaries, plant cells. |
| 37 | Thomas Willis | *Cerebri Anatome* | 1664 | Latin | Neuroanatomy. Circle of Willis, classification of cranial nerves. Rich in anatomical entity names. |
| 38 | Robert Hooke | *Micrographia* | 1665 | English | Coined "cell." Microscopic observations of cork, fleas, mold. Visually stunning—the entity here is the *observable* microworld. |
| 39 | Athanasius Kircher | *Mundus Subterraneus* | 1665 | Latin | Subterranean world: volcanoes, fossils, alchemy, underground rivers, dragons. Encyclopedic Jesuit natural philosophy. |
| 40 | Francesco Redi | *Esperienze Intorno alla Generazione degl'Insetti* | 1668 | Italian | Experimental disproof of spontaneous generation. Important early text of Italian experimental natural philosophy. |
| 41 | Nicolas Lémery | *Cours de Chymie* | 1675 | French | Most popular chemistry textbook of its era (31 editions). Systematic chemical nomenclature. |
| 42 | Thomas Sydenham | *Observationes Medicae* | 1676 | Latin / English | "The English Hippocrates." Nosology—classifying diseases as species. Laudanum, epidemic constitutions. |
| 43 | Nehemiah Grew | *The Anatomy of Plants* | 1682 | English | Plant anatomy via microscope. Pollen, stamens, cellular tissue. Parallel to Malpighi. |
| 44 | John Ray | *Historia Plantarum* | 1686–1704 | Latin | Systematic botany pre-Linnaeus. 18,000+ plant species. His species concept influenced everything after. |
| 45 | Georg Eberhard Rumphius | *Herbarium Amboinense* | comp. 1690s, pub. 1741 | Latin / Dutch | 1,200+ plants of the Moluccas. Rumphius went blind and kept working from memory. Local names preserved alongside Latin and Dutch descriptions. |
| 46 | Pierre Pomet | *Histoire Générale des Drogues* | 1694 | French | Comprehensive guide to drugs and materia medica as commodities. Trade routes, preparation methods, adulteration. |

## 18th Century

| # | Author | Title | Date | Language | Notes |
|---|--------|-------|------|----------|-------|
| 47 | Maria Sibylla Merian | *Metamorphosis Insectorum Surinamensium* | 1705 | Dutch / Latin | Insect life cycles in Suriname. Extraordinary for combining art, observation, and indigenous knowledge (from enslaved and free Africans and Amerindians). |
| 48 | Georg Ernst Stahl | *Theoria Medica Vera* | 1708 | Latin | Animism and phlogiston theory. His chemical terminology (phlogiston, etc.) dominated for 80 years. |
| 49 | Herman Boerhaave | *Institutiones Medicae* | 1708 | Latin | The most influential medical textbook of the 18th century. Iatromechanism—the body as hydraulic machine. Taught all of Europe. |
| 50 | Engelbert Kaempfer | *Amoenitatum Exoticarum* | 1712 | Latin | Japan, Persia, Southeast Asia. Acupuncture, moxa, tea, camphor. First detailed European account of Japanese natural knowledge. |
| 51 | Carl Linnaeus | *Systema Naturae* | 1735 (1st ed.) | Latin | Binomial nomenclature. The naming system itself. Every subsequent entity name in natural history relates to Linnaeus. |
| 52 | Carl Linnaeus | *Species Plantarum* | 1753 | Latin | Starting point of modern botanical nomenclature. 5,900 plant species. |
| 53 | Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon | *Histoire Naturelle, Générale et Particulière* | 1749–88 | French | 36 volumes. Narrative natural history as literary art. Biogeography, deep time, degeneration theory. Immensely influential in concept formation. |
| 54 | Albrecht von Haller | *Elementa Physiologiae Corporis Humani* | 1757–66 | Latin | 8-volume encyclopedia of physiology. Irritability vs. sensibility distinction. The most systematic physiological work before the 19th century. |
| 55 | Giovanni Battista Morgagni | *De Sedibus et Causis Morborum per Anatomen Indagatis* | 1761 | Latin | Founder of pathological anatomy. 700 case histories linking symptoms to organ lesions. |
| 56 | José Celestino Mutis | *Flora de Bogotá* (Royal Botanical Expedition) | 1783–1816 | Spanish / Latin | 20,000+ botanical illustrations from New Granada. Never fully published in his lifetime but the entity data (plant names, indigenous terms) is extraordinary. |
| 57 | Hipólito Ruiz & José Pavón | *Flora Peruvianae et Chilensis* | 1798–1802 | Latin / Spanish | Cinchona (quinine), coca, and hundreds of Andean plants. Spanish botanical expeditions are underrepresented in digital humanities. |
| 58 | Antoine-Laurent de Jussieu | *Genera Plantarum* | 1789 | Latin / French | Natural classification of plants—the system that replaced Linnaeus's artificial one. Foundational for all subsequent taxonomy. |
| 59 | Antoine Lavoisier | *Traité Élémentaire de Chimie* | 1789 | French | Chemical revolution. Oxygen theory of combustion. New nomenclature that replaced phlogiston, calx, etc. with modern element names. |
| 60 | Luigi Galvani | *De Viribus Electricitatis in Motu Musculari* | 1791 | Latin | Animal electricity. Frogs' legs, bioelectricity. Where physics meets physiology. |
| 61 | Erasmus Darwin | *Zoonomia; or, The Laws of Organic Life* | 1794–96 | English | Proto-evolutionary theory. Classification of diseases. Charles Darwin's grandfather—his ideas on transmutation are a direct precursor. |
| 62 | John Hunter | *A Treatise on the Blood, Inflammation, and Gun-Shot Wounds* | 1794 | English | Surgical pathology and experimental physiology. Hunter's museum collection (13,000 specimens) embodied a whole classification system. |
| 63 | Alexander von Humboldt | *Ansichten der Natur* | 1808 | German | Nature as interconnected system. Plant geography, isothermal lines, the concept of "Naturgemälde." Bridge between 18th-c. natural history and 19th-c. ecology. |
| 64 | Carl Peter Thunberg | *Flora Japonica* | 1784 | Latin | Linnaean botany applied to Japanese plants. Thunberg was the only Western naturalist allowed into Japan during its period of isolation. Bridges European and East Asian botanical knowledge in Latin. |

## 19th Century

| # | Author | Title | Date | Language | Notes |
|---|--------|-------|------|----------|-------|
| 65 | Xavier Bichat | *Recherches Physiologiques sur la Vie et la Mort* | 1800 | French | Tissue doctrine—21 tissue types as the fundamental units of anatomy. Life defined as "the totality of functions which resist death." |
| 66 | Jean-Baptiste Lamarck | *Philosophie Zoologique* | 1809 | French | First systematic theory of biological evolution. Inheritance of acquired characters, the "chain of being" transformed into a genealogy. |
| 67 | Johann Wolfgang von Goethe | *Zur Farbenlehre* / *Die Metamorphose der Pflanzen* | 1790 / 1810 | German | Morphology, the Urpflanze, anti-Newtonian color theory. Romantic Naturphilosophie as an alternative epistemology of nature. |
| 68 | Samuel Hahnemann | *Organon der Heilkunst* | 1810 | German | Founding text of homeopathy. "Like cures like," infinitesimal doses, provings. Created a parallel pharmacopoeia with its own entity vocabulary. |
| 69 | Lorenz Oken | *Lehrbuch der Naturphilosophie* | 1809–11 | German | Speculative biology. Vertebral theory of the skull. Represents the Naturphilosophie tradition that shaped German science before positivism. |
| 70 | Georges Cuvier | *Le Règne Animal* | 1817 | French | Four embranchements of the animal kingdom. Comparative anatomy as the key to classification. Anti-evolutionary but foundational for zoology. |
| 71 | Charles Lyell | *Principles of Geology* | 1830–33 | English | Uniformitarianism. Deep geological time. The intellectual precondition for Darwin's theory. Rich in geological and paleontological entities. |
| 72 | Justus von Liebig | *Die Organische Chemie in ihrer Anwendung auf Agricultur und Physiologie* | 1840 | German | Agricultural chemistry, nitrogen cycle, artificial fertilizer. Transformed understanding of metabolism. |
| 73 | Theodor Schwann | *Mikroskopische Untersuchungen* | 1839 | German | Cell theory. All living things are composed of cells. With Schleiden, one of the great unifying concepts in biology. |
| 74 | Carl Gustav Carus | *Psyche: Zur Entwicklungsgeschichte der Seele* | 1846 | German | "The key to the understanding of the nature of the conscious life of the soul lies in the region of the unconscious." Pre-Freudian depth psychology rooted in Romantic natural philosophy. |
| 75 | Alexander von Humboldt | *Kosmos: Entwurf einer Physischen Weltbeschreibung* | 1845–62 | German | 5-volume synthesis of all natural knowledge. The last attempt at a unified description of the physical world. Concept-entity paradise. |
| 76 | Rudolf Virchow | *Die Cellularpathologie* | 1858 | German | "Omnis cellula e cellula." Disease located in cells, not humors. The end of humoral medicine as orthodoxy. |
| 77 | Charles Darwin | *On the Origin of Species* | 1859 | English | Natural selection, descent with modification. Reshaped every entity category: species, variety, genus become fluid, not fixed. |
| 78 | Florence Nightingale | *Notes on Nursing: What It Is, and What It Is Not* | 1859 | English | Professionalization of nursing knowledge. Sanitation, ventilation, diet, observation. Surprisingly entity-rich on disease and environmental concepts. |
| 79 | Gustav Fechner | *Elemente der Psychophysik* | 1860 | German | Mathematical relationship between stimulus and sensation. Founded experimental psychology alongside physiology. |
| 80 | Louis Pasteur | *Mémoire sur les Corpuscules Organisés qui Existent dans l'Atmosphère* | 1861 | French | Disproof of spontaneous generation, germ theory foundations. Pasteur's works collectively transform disease entities from miasmas to microbes. |
| 81 | Herbert Spencer | *Principles of Biology* | 1864 | English | "Survival of the fittest." Social Darwinism's biological foundations. Important for concept entities: evolution, adaptation, organism-as-society. |
| 82 | Claude Bernard | *Introduction à l'Étude de la Médecine Expérimentale* | 1865 | French | Experimental method in medicine. *Milieu intérieur* (internal environment/homeostasis). The manifesto of scientific medicine. |
| 83 | Gregor Mendel | *Versuche über Pflanzenhybriden* | 1866 | German | Hereditary "factors," dominant and recessive traits. Short paper, enormous consequence. Ignored until 1900. |
| 84 | Ernst Haeckel | *Generelle Morphologie der Organismen* | 1866 | German | Coined "ecology," "phylum," "ontogeny recapitulates phylogeny." Darwinism rendered into German morphological tradition. Beautiful illustrations. |
| 85 | Thomas Henry Huxley | *Evidence as to Man's Place in Nature* | 1863 | English | Comparative anatomy of apes and humans. "Darwin's bulldog." Where evolution meets anthropology. |
| 86 | Alfred Russel Wallace | *The Malay Archipelago* | 1869 | English | Biogeography, the Wallace Line, natural selection co-discovery. 8 years of fieldwork. Place and species entities galore. |
| 87 | Santiago Ramón y Cajal | *Textura del Sistema Nervioso del Hombre y de los Vertebrados* | 1899–1904 | Spanish | Neuron doctrine. Mapped the cellular structure of the brain. Nobel Prize 1906. Major Spanish-language scientific text. |
| 88 | Wilhelm Wundt | *Grundzüge der Physiologischen Psychologie* | 1874 | German | Founded the first experimental psychology laboratory. Systematized the vocabulary of sensation, perception, apperception. |
| 89 | Robert Koch | *Die Aetiologie der Tuberculose* | 1882 | German | Koch's postulates. Identification of the tubercle bacillus. Disease entities get specific microbial agents. |
| 90 | Hermann von Helmholtz | *Handbuch der Physiologischen Optik* | 1856–67 | German | Physics of vision: color theory, accommodation, ophthalmoscope. Where physics, physiology, and psychology converge. |
| 91 | Charles Darwin | *The Descent of Man, and Selection in Relation to Sex* | 1871 | English | Human evolution, sexual selection, emotional expression. Extends natural selection to human societies. |
| 92 | Mary Putnam Jacobi | *The Question of Rest for Women During Menstruation* | 1877 | English | Harvard Boylston Prize. Experimental physiology applied to women's health against prevailing dogma. Rare example of women's scientific voice in 19th c. |
| 93 | Emil du Bois-Reymond | *Untersuchungen über Thierische Elektricität* | 1848–84 | German | Nerve impulses as electrical phenomena. Laid the foundation for electrophysiology. His 1880 "Ignorabimus" speech defined the limits of natural science. |
| 94 | William James | *The Principles of Psychology* | 1890 | English | Stream of consciousness, habit, emotion, will. Psychology's transition from philosophy to experimental science. Rich in concept entities. |
| 95 | Félix de Azara | *Apuntamientos para la Historia Natural de los Quadrúpedos del Paraguay y Río de la Plata* | 1802 | Spanish | 20 years of observation in South America. Influenced Darwin and Humboldt. Underappreciated Spanish-language natural history. |
| 96 | Johannes Müller | *Handbuch der Physiologie des Menschen* | 1833–40 | German | The textbook that trained a generation of German physiologists (Helmholtz, Virchow, Schwann, du Bois-Reymond were all his students). Synthesizes vitalism and empirical physiology. |
| 97 | Aimé Bonpland & Alexander von Humboldt | *Essai sur la Géographie des Plantes* | 1807 | French | Plant geography as a science. The famous cross-section of Chimborazo. Altitude, temperature, and vegetation zones. |
| 98 | Peter Simon Pallas | *Reise durch Verschiedene Provinzen des Russischen Reichs* | 1771–76 | German | Natural history of Russia: Siberia, the Urals, the Caspian. Geology, botany, zoology, ethnography. Important for non-Western-European coverage. |
| 99 | Augustin Pyramus de Candolle | *Prodromus Systematis Naturalis Regni Vegetabilis* | 1824–73 | Latin / French | 17-volume attempt to describe every known plant species. The most ambitious taxonomic project of the 19th century. |
| 100 | Georg Forster | *A Voyage Round the World* | 1777 | English (original German ed. 1778–80) | Naturalist on Cook's second voyage. Pacific natural history, ethnography. Humboldt called Forster the greatest influence on his thinking. |

## Notes on Selection Criteria

**Languages represented:** Latin, Italian, French, Spanish, Portuguese, English, Dutch, German

**Prioritized for concordance value:**
- Texts with heavy cross-referencing of earlier authorities (Gessner, Browne, Aldrovandi)
- Multilingual substance/drug lists (Monardes, García de Orta, Pomet, Rumphius)
- Works that introduced new terminology (Paracelsus, Harvey, Lavoisier, Haeckel)
- Colonial natural histories preserving global knowledge in European languages (Sahagún, Hernández, Piso, Merian)
- Transitional texts where old and new nomenclature coexist (Stahl, Bichat, Virchow)

**Notable omissions (already in the concordance or too short):**
- Nicholas Culpeper, *The English Physician* (1652) — already ingested
- João Curvo Semedo, *Polyanthea Medicinal* (1741) — already ingested

**Suggested ingestion priority (high entity density + cross-book linking potential):**
1. Monardes, *Historia Medicinal* — links directly to Culpeper's substance vocabulary
2. García de Orta, *Colóquios* — overlaps with both Semedo (Portuguese) and Culpeper (substances)
3. Dioscorides, *De Materia Medica* — upstream source for nearly every entity in both existing books
4. Gessner, *Historia Animalium* — massive cross-reference network
5. Linnaeus, *Species Plantarum* — the naming backbone for all botanical entities
